FEM & FKM Strength Assessment of a Racing-Car Seat Shell

2026 ANSYS Mechanical Finite Element Analysis
Finite Element Analysis (FEA) ANSYS Fatigue Analysis Failure Analysis Mesh Optimization Stress Analysis ANSYS Mechanical

Overview

This project evaluates the structural integrity of a racing car seat shell designed to protect the driver under extreme loading conditions. The seat must balance lightweighting for vehicle performance with structural rigidity to protect the driver's spine and pelvis during high-performance driving and crash scenarios. The analysis follows the FKM guideline for local stress assessment, combining finite element simulation with standardized strength verification procedures used in German automotive and aerospace engineering.

Objectives

The primary objectives of this project were to:

  • Verify structural adequacy under 9g vertical acceleration (circuit driving)
  • Assess crashworthiness under 20g rear impact with 120kg driver mass
  • Apply FKM guideline for static and fatigue strength assessment
  • Determine degree of utilization and identify failure modes
  • Provide concrete design recommendations for structural improvement
Seat Shell Geometry

Load Case Definitions

The seat was analyzed under 2 different load scenarios:

  • Load Case 1: 9g vertical acceleration
  • Load Case 2: 20g rear impact with 120kg driver mass
  • Fatigue Loads: Cyclic loading representing 10,000 hours of race use

Geometry Simplification

First, the original CAD model of the seat shell was simplified to remove non-structural features (e.g. supports, headrest) while preserving critical load paths and stress concentration areas. The final geometry consisted of a single shell body with uniform thickness, representing the main load-bearing structure of the seat.

Seat Shell Geometry

Material Properties & Meshing

The seat shell was modeled using carbon fiber reinforced polymer (CFRP) with orthotropic material properties validated through experimental testing. A structured mesh convergence study was performed to ensure solution accuracy (>70% element quality) while maintaining computational efficiency.

Course Mesh of the seat

FKM Strength Assessment

Following FKM guidelines, safety factors were calculated for static and fatigue loading. The analysis considered:

  • Material strength reduction factors for CFRP layup quality
  • Surface finish and stress concentration factors
  • Load spectrum and mean stress effects for fatigue
  • Safety factors appropriate for racing applications

Analysis Workflow

The analysis workflow consisted of:

  • Phase 1: Linear Elastic - Baseline stress distribution, identify hotspots, verify load paths
  • Phase 2: Elastic-Ideally Plastic - Capture yielding behavior, determine plastic collapse load
  • Phase 3: FKM Assessment - Calculate degree of utilization per guideline

Results and Analysis

Load Case 1: 9g Vertical Acceleration

Circuit driving scenario with vertical acceleration equivalent to 9g acting on the driver mass (120 kg) through the seat contact patch.

Boundary Conditions
Static Analysis:

Applied the FKM guideline (Chapters 3–4) for static strength verification using local stresses extracted from the linear-elastic model. The calculated degree of utilization of 0.71 indicates the component operates within allowable limits under this load case, with approximately 29% safety margin before reaching the design limit.

Load Case 1: 9g Vertical Acceleration
Fatigue Analysis:

The fatigue assessment for 10⁸ load cycles did not meet the required endurance level. While the seat shell satisfies static strength requirements, repeated loading at 9g vertical acceleration would lead to fatigue failure before reaching the target service life.

Design Recommendations

Increase thickness in high-stress regions or introduce local reinforcements (ribs, doublers) to reduce stress concentration and improve fatigue life

Load Case 2: 20g Rear Crash

Rear impact scenario with 20g deceleration acting through the driver's back and head contact areas.

Equivalent Stress Distribution
Analysis Findings:

The linear-elastic simulation produced unrealistically high local stresses, indicating the structure was driven far beyond the elastic range. The elastic-ideally-plastic model confirmed early structural failure — the critical plastic strain criterion was reached at only a small fraction of the full 20g load. The seat shell does not satisfy the static strength requirement for the 20g rear crash case. Plastic collapse occurs well before reaching the design load, indicating a fundamental structural inadequacy.

Design Recommendations

Major redesign required. Consider:

  • (1) increased shell thickness in back support region
  • (2) structural ribs or stiffeners along load path
  • (3) integration of energy-absorbing elements
  • (4) material upgrade to higher-strength alloy (e.g., Al7075-T651 with improved damage tolerance)

Conclusions

  • Load Case 1 (9g vertical): Passes static strength check with 29% safety margin (degree of utilization = 0.71), but fails the 10⁸-cycle fatigue requirement.
  • Load Case 2 (20g rear crash): Fails static strength check due to plastic collapse at loads significantly below the design requirement.
The analysis confirms that the seat shell requires structural redesign before it can be considered acceptable for the specified service conditions. Priority should be given to addressing the 20g crash case failure through thickness increases, reinforcement, or material upgrade, followed by fatigue optimization for the 9g circuit-driving scenario.
